Solar System

Solar System is centered around the Sun, the shining ball in the sky. It includes the family of eight planets (excluding
Pluto which is a dwarf planet since 2006) orbiting (traveling around) the Sun, as well as the moons of these planets, and smaller objects, such as comets, asteroids, and bits of space rock. The powerful pull of an invisible force called gravity from the Sun stops these bodies from flying off into deepest space.

Sun

Sun, a blazing ball of hot, ionized gases, dominates our solar system, providing life-sustaining light and heat to Earth. Its powerful gravitational pull orchestrates the celestial dance of planets. A cosmic fusion reactor, the sun's core reaches temperatures of millions of degrees, fueling the radiant energy that shapes our solar environment.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and closest planet to the Sun in our solar system, with no atmosphere to retain heat. Its surface temperature ranges from extremely hot to frigid cold. Mercury's dayside can reach scorching temperatures, while its nightside experiences extreme cold. It completes an orbit around the Sun in just 88 Earth days.

Venus

Venus, the second planet from the sun, is Earth's closest neighbor. Known for its scorching surface temperatures due to a thick atmosphere trapping heat, it is often called Earth's "sister planet." Venus rotates slowly, and its dense, cloudy atmosphere is composed mainly of carbon dioxide. It's a captivating celestial body in our solar system.

Earth

Earth, the third planet from the sun in our solar system, sustains life with a diverse biosphere. Its atmosphere, primarily nitrogen and oxygen, supports a delicate balance, and vast oceans cover its surface. Home to a rich tapestry of ecosystems, Earth is the cradle of human civilization and a marvel in the cosmos.

Moon

Moon, Earth's only natural satellite, captivates with its silvery glow in the night sky. It influences tides, its phases marking the passage of time. Scientists explore its mysteries, while poets find inspiration in its serene beauty. The moon, a celestial companion, holds cultural significance across diverse civilizations, sparking wonder and curiosity.

Mars

Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is a cold, desert-like world with a thin atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide. It features prominent surface features like the largest volcano, Olympus Mons, and the extensive Valles Marineris canyon system. Scientists explore its potential for past or present microbial life and future human colonization.

Jupiter

Jupiter, the largest planet in our solar system, is a gas giant with a massive atmosphere primarily composed of hydrogen and helium. It boasts a strong magnetic field and iconic Great Red Spot, a giant storm. Jupiter has numerous moons, including the four largest known as the Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.

Saturn

Saturn, the sixth planet from the Sun, is known for its stunning ring system composed of ice and rock particles. It is a gas giant, primarily consisting of hydrogen and helium. With a prominent set of rings and numerous moons, including Titan, Saturn is a captivating celestial body in our solar system.

Uranus

Uranus, the seventh planet from the sun, is an ice giant in our solar system. It's unique for its extreme axial tilt, causing it to rotate on its side. Discovered in 1781 by William Herschel, Uranus has a bluish hue due to methane in its atmosphere. It remains a fascinating celestial body for scientific study.

Neptune

Neptune, the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun in our solar system, is a gas giant with a vivid blue hue due to methane in its atmosphere. Discovered in 1846, it has a turbulent atmosphere featuring prominent storms, including the Great Dark Spot. Neptune has a unique retrograde orbit and multiple moons.

Pluto

Pluto, once considered the ninth planet in our solar system, was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006. It orbits the Sun in the distant Kuiper Belt, characterized by icy bodies. Discovered in 1930, Pluto has a complex atmosphere and five known moons. Its reclassification sparked debates about the definition of a planet.
